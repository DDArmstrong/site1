Health Care: Who Needs It? Who Has it?

When it comes to healthcare, there's a gap between supply and demand. And that gap varies by location.

The demand depends on the number of unhealthy people and their ability to pay for care. The supply includes things like the number of doctors and healthcare facilities.. Gaps occur when there's a big difference between what people need (demand) and what they can get (supply).

Health Care Demand

See the chart

The University of Wisconsin compiles a dataset called County Health Rankings. There's a variable called "poor or fair health", which is the percentage of adults reporting fair or poor health. The higher this percentage, the greater the demand for healthcare. According to this metric, the healthiest states are Vermont, New Hampshire and Iowa; the unhealthiest are West Virgina, Arkansas and Mississippi.





Insurance

See the chart
(Bar chart by state)

The percentage of uninsured is also an indicator of demand: Those with insurance are more likely to be able to pay for health services. Unfortunately, it those in the unhealthiest states are also least able to pay due to low rates of insurance coverage.

Those with higher incomes also tend to be healthier. This reveals another gap: Those with the fewest health problems are most able to pay for it.

Healthcare Workers

Healthcare doesn't just come from doctors. Occupations ranging from nurse practitioners to nutritionists to dentists also provice essential services. The higher the proportion of healthcare workers, the greater the availability of healthcare.
